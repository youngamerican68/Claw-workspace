## 4. NUTRITION & FASTING

**Timing Strategies**:

- **Coffee Timing**: Avoid caffeine for the first 90 minutes after waking.

**Foods to Prioritize**:
- **Water**: Essential for hydration, especially upon waking.
- **Coconut Water**: Recommended as a last-resort hangover remedy due to its zinc, magnesium, and B vitamin content.

**What to Limit**:
- **Alcohol**: No healthy level of alcohol consumption. Even low levels increase cancer risk (breast, esophageal). Moderate to heavy drinking increases risk across many cancer types, liver disease, and brain damage. "I would never say drinking alcohol is good for your health." (Dr. Michael Bruce)
- **Sugar Before Bed**: Sugar slows melatonin production, making it harder to initiate sleep.
- **Caffeine Late in Day**: Stop caffeine consumption by 2 PM. The half-life of caffeine is 6-8 hours, so it can disrupt sleep if consumed later.
- **Highly Processed Foods**: Contribute to obesity, which is a major driver of sleep apnea and liver disease.
- **Processed Meats**: Increase the risk of colon cancer.

## 5. SLEEP OPTIMIZATION

**Pre-Sleep Protocols**:
- **One-Hour Wind-Down**: Dedicate the hour before bed to a structured wind-down:
    - **First 20 minutes**: Address "gotta do" tasks (e.g., school bags, work prep, sending last email).
    - **Second 20 minutes**: Hygiene (brush teeth, wash face, shower).
    - **Final 20 minutes**: Calming activity (meditation, relaxation, prayer).
- **Bedtime Alarm**: Set an alarm to remind yourself to start your wind-down routine.
- **Progressive Muscle Relaxation**: Tense and relax muscles from feet upwards to release tension and promote relaxation. (Audio file available from Dr. Michael Bruce).

**Sleep Environment**:
- **Cool Temperature**: Sleep in a cool environment. Core body temperature drops during sleep, and a cool room facilitates this. "You want to sleep in the cool, not the warm environment." (Dr. Michael Bruce)
- **Temperature Control Devices**: Consider products like the Ooler Sleep System to actively cool/heat the bed to align with circadian rhythms.
- **Darkness**: Use blackout curtains or a sleep mask to block light. Travel with a sleep mask to mitigate "first night effect" in new environments.
- **Quiet**: Aim for a quiet environment. If noise is unavoidable, white noise or specific earbuds (like NextSense) can help.
- **Air Quality**: Use an air purifier in the bedroom. Open windows weekly for fresh air (unless allergies are an issue).
- **Aromatherapy**: Use diffusers, sachets, or pillow mists with lavender or ylang-ylang (scientifically shown to be helpful for sleep). Avoid candles due to fire risk.
- **Sleeping Naked**: Thermoregulates better with less clothing.
- **Pillow Quality**: Choose a pillow that keeps your nose in line with your sternum to prevent neck pain and improve deep sleep. Consider firmness preference (squishy vs. firm) and gusseted designs for neck support. Adjustable pillows (e.g., shredded latex with zipper) are ideal.
- **Sleep Position**: The "worst position" is stomach sleeping due to pressure on the lower back. The "best position" is sleeping on your left side to prevent gastric reflux.

**Supplements**:
- **Magnesium**: Critical for sleep, especially if deficient. Dr. Michael Bruce recommends Magnesium 3.08 (Magtine) or Upgraded Formulas' magnesium. Banana tea is a natural source.
- **Vitamin D**: A "circadian pacemaker" that helps regulate melatonin. Take daily or get 15 minutes of sunshine.
- **GABA (Gamma-Aminobutyric Acid)**: A supplement that acts as the brain's "brakes" and can help calm people down in the evening.
- **Valerian Root**: An anti-anxiety root that can help lower anxiety, often combined with hops for better effect.
- **Melatonin (Cautionary Use)**:
    - **Dose**: Half a milligram to 1.5 mg, maximum 3 mg. High doses (10-20 mg) can cause "crazy dreams."
    - **Purpose**: A sleep regulator, not an initiator. Affects sleep rhythm, not sleep drive.
    - **Use Cases**: Jet lag, shift work, melatonin deficiency (common after age 50), autism spectrum disorder (5-7 mg).
    - **Contraindications**: Interacts with SSRIs, birth control, blood pressure medication, and diabetes medication. Not for use in children (except specific cases like autism spectrum disorder) due to potential impact on puberty and teaching reliance on pills.
    - **Quality**: FDA does not regulate melatonin in the US; actual content can vary wildly from label.

**Troubleshooting**:
- **Middle-of-the-Night Awakenings**:
    - **Don't go pee**: Unless absolutely necessary. Going from lying to standing increases heart rate. If side sleeping, try lying on your back for 25 seconds to relieve bladder pressure.
    - **Don't check phone/clock**: Seeing the time causes mental math and anxiety, disrupting sleep. Blue light from screens also signals morning to the brain.
    - **Use 4-7-8 breathing**: To lower heart rate and distract the mind.
    - **Positive Self-Talk**: Replace negative thoughts ("I can't sleep") with positive affirmations ("I'm okay, I'm just going to relax and let sleep take over").
    - **Non-Sleep Deep Rest (NSDR)**: If still awake, lie relaxed in a "corpse pose." 1 hour of NSDR is equivalent to 20 minutes of sleep.
    - **Get out of bed**: If anxiety increases, go to another room with a book and soft light to read until sleepy, then return to bed. Avoid associating the bed with anxiety.
- **Irregular Sleep Schedule**: Wake up at the same time seven days a week. "The time that you wake up directly determines when your internal melatonin kicks into gear." (Dr. Michael Bruce)
- **Parenting and Sleep**:
    - **Educate children**: Help them understand the importance of sleep for their activities.
    - **Set guidelines**: Establish consistent bedtimes and wake-up times.
    - **"On-call" method**: Parents take turns being responsible for nighttime awakenings to allow one parent to get uninterrupted sleep.

## 7. KEY DEBATES

**The Question**: Is there a "safe" or "healthy" amount of alcohol consumption?

**Different Positions**:
- **Traditional View (J-shaped curve)**: "People who are drinking even up to the moderate level were actually doing better than the people who weren't drinking at all." (Dr. Michael Bruce) This view suggested that moderate drinkers had lower health risks than non-drinkers, implying a health benefit.
- **Updated View (No Safe Amount)**: "I would never say drinking alcohol is good for your health." (Dr. Michael Bruce) This perspective argues that once the "non-drinker" reference group is refined to exclude those who abstain due to existing health issues or recovery, the perceived health benefits disappear. Alcohol is now linked to increased risk for various cancers (breast, esophageal even at low levels; others at moderate/heavy levels) and other health issues with any consumption.

**Practical Takeaway**:
- **Acknowledge Risks**: Understand that alcohol consumption, even at low levels, carries health risks and is not health-promoting.
- **Low-Risk Guidelines**: For those who choose to drink, adhere to very low-risk guidelines (e.g., fewer than 14 UK units/week, or 7 US standard drinks/week). Be aware that "one drink" can often be multiple units/standard drinks.
- **Minimize Heavy/Binge Drinking**: Heavy drinking episodes cause significant spikes in toxic byproducts that stress the liver and other organs.
- **Consider Individual Factors**: Genetic predisposition, existing health conditions, and individual metabolism can significantly alter risk.
